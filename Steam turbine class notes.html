01 What is a Steam Turbine

Lecture 8, p.1–2

Definition: A mechanical device that extracts thermal energy from pressurized steam and converts it into rotary motion.

  • Rotary motion → ideal for driving an electrical generator.
  • ~80% of world electricity is generated using steam turbines.
EXAM FACT: 80%

02 Two Functions of a Turbine

Lecture 8, p.3

A steam turbine uses steam as working fluid and does two jobs:

  • (a) Nozzle: Pressure energy → Kinetic energy (steam expands through nozzles).
  • (b) Moving blades: Kinetic energy (+ remaining pressure energy, if any) → Mechanical work.
Steam (pressure energy) → [Nozzle] → Kinetic energy → [Moving blades] → Mechanical work
CORE CONCEPT — everything else builds on this

03 Real Plant Layout & Reheat Cycle

Lecture 8, p.4–5

Practical turbines are staged on one shaft: HP turbine → LP turbine → Generator (simple layout), or in large plants:

Main header → Stop/Control valves → HP turbine → (steam sent to boiler reheater) → Reheat stop/Intercept valve → Crossover → IP turbine → LP turbine → Condenser
  • Feedwater heaters tap off steam along the way to preheat boiler feedwater (extraction).
  • This is called a reheat cycle — improves plant efficiency.

05 Pressure Heads

Lecture 8, p.7, 9

Head relates a fluid's energy to the height of an equivalent static column of that fluid (unit: meters or feet, not Pa).

Kinetic energy = ½ m v² Potential energy = m g h
  • Static fluid (zero velocity): p/(ρg) + z = H (pressure head + elevation head only)
  • Flowing fluid: velocity head is added too — full Bernoulli form (see next section)
  • Total head line includes velocity head; hydraulic grade line does not.

07 Impulse Turbine

Lecture 8, p.10–11 · Lecture 10, p.2–4

Steam from the boiler passes through fixed nozzles and strikes the moving blades. The impulse (push) of steam rotates the wheel → shaft power. Kinetic energy → mechanical energy.

  • In the nozzle: velocity increases sharply, pressure drops sharply.
  • Across moving blades: pressure stays constant, velocity decreases gradually (KE → work). This is the defining feature of impulse turbines.
  • Force on the wheel comes from the change in direction/velocity of steam across the blade (momentum change).
  • Steam stays in contact with atmosphere continuously.
Key graph: Pressure drops only in the nozzle; velocity rises in nozzle then falls across the blades toward condenser pressure.
Pressure drop = NOZZLE ONLY

08 Pelton Wheel

Lecture 8, p.12 · Lecture 10, p.5

Example of an impulse turbine (uses water as fluid).

  • Wheel mounted on a shaft with buckets on its periphery.
  • Water impinges on buckets, transferring energy.
  • Water has only kinetic energy at this point (all pressure energy already converted in the nozzle).
  • Each bucket is shaped like a double hemispherical cup.
  • Used for high head: 150–2000 m.

09 Reaction Turbine

Lecture 10, p.6–8

Constructed of rows of fixed blades (stator) and moving blades (rotor). The fixed blades themselves form convergent nozzles — the turbine uses the reaction force from steam accelerating through them.

  • At entrance: only part of the energy → KE, the rest stays as pressure energy.
  • The fluid exerts a reaction force opposite to its flow direction (Newton's 3rd law).
Key exam sentence: "A pressure drop occurs across both the stator and the rotor, with steam accelerating through the stator and decelerating through the rotor, with no net change in steam velocity across the stage but with a decrease in both pressure and temperature."

Three forces driving a reaction turbine:

  • Reactive force from steam expanding through the nozzle-shaped moving-blade passages
  • Reactive force from the gas changing direction on the blades
  • Direct push/impulse of gas hitting the blades
Conclusion: A reaction turbine moves primarily by reactive force, but also partly by direct impulse.
Pressure drop = BOTH stator AND rotor

12 Compounding of Impulse Turbine

Lecture 11, p.5

If all the steam's high velocity is absorbed by just one row of moving blades, the rotor would need to spin at about 30,000 rpm — far too high for practical use (excess centrifugal force → mechanical failure).

To fix this, more than one set of nozzles and rotors are used in series, keyed to the same shaft, so pressure or velocity is absorbed gradually in stages. This is called compounding (staging).

  • Velocity compounding — also called Curtis staging
  • Pressure compounding — also called Rateau staging
Either method (or both combined) reduces the impractically high rotational speed of a single-stage turbine.
EXAM FACT: single stage → ~30,000 rpm (too high)

13 Pressure-Compounding (Rateau Turbine)

Lecture 11, p.6
  • The total enthalpy drop through the nozzles is divided (roughly equally) among many single-stage impulse turbines in series — this is called a Rateau turbine.
  • Inlet steam velocity to each stage is essentially equal.
  • Pressure drop happens across more than one row of nozzles; kinetic energy gained after each nozzle is kept limited.
  • Usually convergent nozzles are used.
Pattern: N–M–N–M–N–M... (Nozzle, Moving blade, repeated) — pressure drops step-by-step at each nozzle row, then stays constant across each moving-blade row.

14 Velocity-Compounding (Curtis Stage)

Lecture 11, p.7
  • The Curtis stage turbine has one stage of nozzles (like the single-stage turbine), followed by two or more rows of moving blades instead of one.
  • These moving-blade rows are separated by a row of fixed blades attached to the stator — their job is to redirect steam from the first row of moving blades to the second row.
  • Total pressure drop occurs only in the nozzle — so pressure stays constant across all three blade rows (moving, fixed, moving).
  • Velocity is absorbed in two stages. In the fixed (static) blade row, both pressure and velocity stay constant — fixed blades here only redirect flow. Fixed blades are also called guide vanes.
Pattern: N–M–F–M (Nozzle, Moving, Fixed/guide vane, Moving) — all pressure drop in the nozzle; velocity drops gradually across the two moving rows.
Rateau = pressure staged; Curtis = velocity staged — don't mix these up

15 Combined Pressure-Velocity Compounding

Lecture 11, p.8
  • In velocity compounding, the total enthalpy drop converts to kinetic energy in a single stage, but that kinetic energy converts to mechanical energy across many blade rows. Fixed blades simply redirect steam to the next row.
  • A series of velocity-compounded impulse stages (i.e., several Curtis stages placed one after another) is called a pressure-velocity compounded turbine.

16 Reaction Turbine — Parson's Turbine

Lecture 11, p.9
Combined impulse-reaction turbines are known as Parson's reaction turbine.
  • Moving blades of a reaction turbine differ from impulse turbine blades: they are not symmetrical. Because they act partly as nozzles, their shape resembles the fixed blades, but curved in the opposite direction.
  • Pressure continuously drops through all rows of blades — both fixed and moving (unlike impulse, where pressure drops only in the nozzle).
  • The absolute steam velocity changes within each stage and repeats from stage to stage (rises then falls, repeatedly, across F-M-F-M... rows).
Reaction blades ≠ symmetrical; impulse blades = symmetrical bucket shape

17 Governing of Steam Turbine

Lecture 11, p.10

Governing = the method used to regulate steam supply to the turbine, needed to keep turbine speed constant regardless of load. Turbine performance depends heavily on the governing method used.

Common governing methods:

  • Throttle governing — steam flow is throttled (restricted) via a valve before entering the turbine
  • Nozzle controlled governing — different sets of nozzles are opened/closed by valves to control steam admission
  • Combined throttle-nozzle governing
  • By-pass governing
Throttle governing uses a stop valve + throttling control valves on a single steam path. Nozzle controlled governing uses multiple nozzle sets (1st, 2nd, 3rd...) fed through separate valves, opened as needed for load.

18 Steam Turbine — Advantages over Gas Turbine

Lecture 11, p.11
  • Steam turbine works on the Rankine cycle, with efficiency close to a Carnot cycle. Gas turbine works on the Brayton cycle, with efficiency much lower than Carnot cycle between the same temperature limits.
  • At part load, steam turbine efficiency doesn't drop much; gas turbine part-load efficiency drops significantly.
  • Blade material cost is considerably less for steam turbines compared to gas turbines.
  • Load control is easier for steam turbines — usually done via throttle governing or governors.
4 advantages: cycle efficiency, part-load efficiency, blade cost, easier load control
